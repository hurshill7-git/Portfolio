<Section eyebrow="01 · Context & problem" title="Two needs pulling in the same direction">

Jeeves had two things to sort out at once. The product itself was a little confusing in places (settings, asset management, finding things), and users needed clear guidance to adopt features without calling for help.

<InPlainTerms>
Jeeves teaches hospital staff how to use their EMR (the electronic medical record, the software clinicians live in all day). The trick is "just-in-time" help: little lessons that appear right when someone is stuck, without making them stop work and go hunting through a manual. Four kinds of people use it: Learners, Authors, Publishers, and Admins.
</InPlainTerms>

In a hospital, "go read the documentation later" is not really an option. The help had to meet people in the moment.

</Section>

<Section eyebrow="02 · Role & process" title="Audit first, then build the help">

I ran a product UI audit (inventoried the components, checked the Settings and Assets flows against real tasks), then built a structured library of 15+ in-product tutorials, each with scripted voiceover. I also designed the marketing-site assets and customer-story pages.

</Section>

<Section eyebrow="03 · Key decisions" title="Decisions toward trust and consistency">

<Decision n="01" title="Reworked the Assets screen">

**What.** Proposed a clearer "add asset" button and row-level states (including a loud red one for things that need attention) for teams juggling big content libraries.

**Why.** Training teams manage hundreds of assets, and the old screen made it weirdly hard to add new content or see, at a glance, what was on fire.

</Decision>

<Decision n="02" title="Added a Settings change log">

**What.** Proposed an auditable record of what changed, who changed it, and when.

**Why.** Healthcare is heavily regulated. Admins need to prove who touched which setting, so a paper trail is not a nice-to-have, it is the job.

</Decision>

<Decision n="03" title="One format for every tutorial">

**What.** Standardized a single template and one voiceover style across the whole library.

**Why.** If every tutorial looks and sounds like a different person made it, people quietly stop trusting them. Sameness, here, is what builds confidence.

</Decision>

</Section>

<Section eyebrow="06 · Reflection" title="A systems problem in two disciplines">

Jeeves stretched me across product UX and content design at the same time. Designing a help system (one template, one voice, mapped to every release) was a systems problem, and the Settings and Assets work pulled me into enterprise concerns like auditability and role-based needs. Both are exactly the kind of unglamorous detail that makes regulated software trustworthy.
