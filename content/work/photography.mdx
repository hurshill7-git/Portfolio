<Section eyebrow="01 · Statement" title="A slice of time and light">

I work across commercial product photography and personal conceptual work. The practice is about how light, composition, and timing carry feeling. A photograph is a thin slice of time and light, and the craft is knowing which slice is worth keeping, then framing it so someone else feels what you felt when you saw it.

</Section>

<Section eyebrow="02 · Body of work" title="Personal and commercial">

My personal work circles a few themes: solitude, light, architecture, people in space. Color and black-and-white, cities and nature. Each series usually starts as a question ("what does early light do to an empty room?") and gets answered through careful framing and a lot of patient waiting.

Commercial work (products, events, headshots) taught me precision and speed. You get one shot at the shot, so you know your gear, your light, and your frame cold. Different pressure, same foundation.

</Section>

<Section eyebrow="03 · Craft" title="Light, composition, timing">

**Light.** Morning light has a quality noon never will. Past the obvious golden hour, I love flat overcast light (no shadows, even and gentle) and the blue hour just after sunset. Hard or soft, direct or bounced, warm or cool, light is what reveals texture, sets the mood, and steers the eye.

<InPlainTerms>
A quick glossary, since photographers hoard jargon: "golden hour" is the warm light just after sunrise or before sunset, "blue hour" is the cool glow just after the sun is gone, and "raking light" is a low, sideways light that grazes a surface to show off its texture.
</InPlainTerms>

**Composition.** Rule of thirds, leading lines, a frame within the frame, but mostly intuition. Slightly off-center often beats dead-center, symmetry is calm, asymmetry is lively. The frame is your first edit: what you leave out decides what the photo is even about.

**Timing.** Being in the right spot at the right moment. I will return to a place across different hours and seasons and just wait, sometimes for a while. Patience teaches you to see before you shoot, and to anticipate instead of spray-and-pray.

<Section eyebrow="04 · What photography teaches design" title="Carried into the work">

Constraints help (a fixed lens forces you to move your feet and think). Details decide everything (a good photo becomes a great one over the tilt of a head or one beat of light). The best composition is invisible, it just feels natural, and that ease is the hard part. And authenticity reads, in photos and in design alike: an honest moment lands harder than something built to impress.

</Section>
