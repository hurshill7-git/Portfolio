<Section eyebrow="01 · Context & problem" title="One product wearing three different outfits">

Jeeves shows up everywhere: in-product help videos, the marketing site, sales decks, customer-story pages, release notes, LinkedIn posts, even printed handouts. When I started, each one had its own idea of the brand. The colors drifted, the fonts disagreed, the logo had several haircuts, and the tutorial videos all paced differently.

<InPlainTerms>
A "design system" is just a shared rulebook for how a brand looks and behaves: the exact colors, fonts, icons, buttons, and motion, plus when to use each one. It exists so designers stop re-deciding the same things and everything ends up looking related.
</InPlainTerms>

The mess cost twice. It blurred the brand (the video, the website, and the deck felt like three companies), and it slowed everyone down, because nobody could reuse anything.

<Callout>
This was a design-system problem, not a coat of paint. The real goal: make the consistent choice the easiest choice, so people reach for it without being nagged.
</Callout>

</Section>

<Section eyebrow="02 · Role & process" title="Own the whole rulebook">

I owned all of it: logo, color, typography, icons, components, motion, and the rules for using them everywhere.

1. **Audit** every existing Jeeves asset across product, web, video, and marketing
2. **Research** the positioning (in-product training for clinicians, with ease of use as the edge)
3. **System design** of one coherent visual language
4. **Documentation** with clear rules, examples, and do's and don'ts
5. **Application** across product UI, the website, videos, and decks
6. **Iteration** as new features and channels appeared

</Section>

<Section eyebrow="03 · Key decisions" title="Decisions that made the system hold">

<Decision n="01" title="A logo system, not a single logo">

**What.** Three variants (full wordmark plus mark, mark only, monochrome) with rules for spacing and minimum size.

**Why.** Jeeves needs to look right on a homepage and in a tiny browser-tab icon and on a one-color print job. No single logo survives all three; a system does.

**Tradeoff.** A system takes more documenting and more discipline to maintain than one file.

**Result.** The logo works confidently everywhere because people follow rules instead of guessing.

</Decision>

<Decision n="02" title="A color palette built for healthcare">

**What.** Five meaningful colors plus neutrals, each with a job (brand blue, success green, warning orange, error red, info purple), all readable to the standard and safe for colorblind users.

<InPlainTerms>
"WCAG AA" is the common accessibility bar for color contrast, basically, a guarantee that text and colors are easy enough to read for people with low vision or color blindness. In healthcare, where color can signal something urgent, that is not optional.
</InPlainTerms>

**Why.** In data-dense medical screens, color carries meaning, so the palette has to be deliberate and legible, not decorative.

**Tradeoff.** Five plus neutrals means fewer options, but consistency is the whole point.

**Result.** Every screen, thumbnail, and graphic shares the same palette, and customers recognize Jeeves on sight.

</Decision>

<Decision n="03" title="One motion language for the videos">

**What.** Standardized the easing, timing, and durations across all 15+ tutorial videos.

**Why.** Motion is part of a brand's personality. When one video is snappy and the next is sluggish, they feel like different products. Matching the motion makes the whole library feel like one helpful voice.

**Tradeoff.** Everyone making videos has to follow the same rules.

**Result.** The tutorial library reads as a single, cohesive help system.

</Decision>

</Section>

<Section eyebrow="04 · Solution & artifacts" title="What the system documents">

- **Logo system:** three variants, clear space, minimum sizes, do's and don'ts
- **Color:** five semantic colors plus neutrals, accessible, with colorblind-safe alternates
- **Typography:** Inter, a five-step size scale, four weights, with clear usage rules
- **Icons:** 40+ custom icons (consistent stroke and size) plus rules for new ones
- **Components:** buttons, cards, inputs, alerts, each documented with their states
- **Motion:** standard easing and durations for micro, UI, and larger transitions
- **Application guides:** product, web, video, print, social, and collateral

<Section eyebrow="06 · Reflection" title="Consistency is a multiplier">

One blue, repeated across 15 videos, a website, a product, and a stack of marketing, builds a recognition no single gorgeous design could. Design systems are living things: strict enough to stay coherent, loose enough to grow. And a rulebook only matters if people actually open it, which happens when following it is easier than ignoring it. Next time I would pull in engineers and marketers earlier, because co-writing the rules is what makes people follow them.
