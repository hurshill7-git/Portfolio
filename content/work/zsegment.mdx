<Section eyebrow="01 · Context & problem" title="A brilliant engine with a baffling dashboard">

ZSegment is heavy-duty software, and the engine under it was genuinely good. The experience on top of it was not. Filters lived in two different places, so people never knew which to use. Empty screens told you nothing. Icons came from three different families that had clearly never met. Whole areas, like billing, had no real design at all.

<InPlainTerms>
Think of ZSegment as the plumbing that lets a hospital's many computer systems talk to each other and move patient data around safely. The people using it ("operators") spend all day routing and checking that data. My job was the dashboard they stare at, not the pipes themselves.
</InPlainTerms>

The result was predictable: extra clicks, support tickets full of "where do I click?", and a first week on the job that every new operator spent quietly lost.

<Callout>
The product was not missing features. It was missing a mental model, a way to actually understand what you were looking at.
</Callout>

</Section>

<Section eyebrow="02 · Role & process" title="Sole designer, soup to nuts">

I owned research, information architecture (how the product is organized and labeled), interaction, and visual design, and I designed the marketing site and comparison page too.

1. **Research and alignment.** Ran a user-persona study with PM Soumya and walked through the product flow by flow with PM and engineers.
2. **Information architecture.** Rebuilt the menu and navigation with the engineering leads, and wrote out the user flow before drawing a single screen.
3. **Design system.** Standardized a single 16x16 icon set, fixed the typography rules, and turned error and empty states into one consistent system.
4. **Iteration.** Tested with internal stakeholders and refined.

</Section>

<Section eyebrow="03 · Key decisions" title="Three decisions that removed the confusion">

<Decision n="01" title="Put all the filters in one place">

**What.** Moved every filter into the left sidebar instead of splitting them between the table header and the sidebar.

**Why.** Dense tables need one source of truth. With filters in two spots, people set one, missed the other, and ran half a query without realizing it.

**Tradeoff.** A wider sidebar leaves less room for the table, which gets tight on small monitors.

**Result.** Operators told us filtering finally makes sense, and the "where are the filters?" tickets stopped.

</Decision>

<Decision n="02" title="Rebuilt the screen people live in">

**What.** Redesigned the messages module (where operators spend most of their day). One search bar instead of a confusing "basic vs advanced" split, filters on top of the table, a save-view button, and the important data promoted up so it is not buried three taps deep.

<InPlainTerms>
A "message" here is a packet of patient data moving between systems, and a "segment" is one field inside it (like a date or an ID). Operators dig through these all day, so every extra tap is a tiny tax on their time.
</InPlainTerms>

**Why.** Roughly 60% of an operator's day is in this one screen. The old version hid the useful bits and made people re-type the same query every morning like a bad Groundhog Day loop.

**Tradeoff.** Expanding the segments leaves the message body less room, which we softened by letting people collapse it.

**Result.** Operators find segments about 3x faster, 85% of them adopted the save-view feature within two weeks, and "where is this in the drawer?" tickets dropped to zero.

</Decision>

<Decision n="03" title="Designed five honest empty states">

**What.** Built a distinct empty state for every "nothing here yet" moment (no interface defined, no source data, no targets, no messages, no schema selected).

**Why.** The old product just showed a blank screen, which left people wondering if they broke something, if data was still loading, or if the feature even applied to them.

**Tradeoff.** Five states take time to design, but once defined, engineers reused the pattern everywhere.

**Result.** Confusion tickets fell. One operator put it best: "Now I know what to do next instead of feeling lost."

</Decision>

</Section>

<Section eyebrow="04 · Solution & artifacts" title="The restructured product">

- Redesigned navigation and information architecture
- A reworked messages module with a cleaner drawer and one unified search
- A complete five-state empty-state system
- One standardized 16x16 icon set, used product-wide
- Billing, subscription, and environment-settings flows
- Onboarding screens for new operators
- The marketing site, a "ZSegment vs Mirth Connect" comparison page, and a 3D product visual

<InPlainTerms>
Mirth Connect is the older, well-known competitor in this space. The comparison page is simply the "here is why we are easier to live with" pitch.

<Section eyebrow="06 · Reflection" title="Design systems compound">

One icon or one filter pattern sounds trivial. Multiplied across hundreds of dense screens, that consistency becomes a usability multiplier. I would build the system even earlier next time.

Owning everything from the product UI to the marketing site taught me they reinforce each other: the clarity I designed into the messages module became a literal selling point in the pitch. And "I actually understand the drawer now" validated the work as much as any chart could.
