<Section eyebrow="01 · Context" title="Great products, forgettable boxes">

A sustainable lifestyle brand needed a real identity. The products were genuinely good (effective, natural, responsibly sourced), but the packaging looked like everything else, so on a shelf it simply vanished.

<InPlainTerms>
The challenge was the "three-second test." On a busy shelf, a shopper decides in about three seconds whether to even pick something up. The brand needed to win that glance, feel worth its premium price, and still look authentically sustainable instead of fake-earthy.
</InPlainTerms>

The trap in this category is that every "natural" brand looks identical: muted earth tones, a hand-drawn leaf, a friendly serif. This one had to stand out without abandoning its values.

</Section>

<Section eyebrow="02 · Approach" title="Strategy into a visual language">

1. **Research:** studied competitors and shoppers, and interviewed the founder about values and the target customer
2. **Visual direction:** organic through the materials, modern through the typography and layout
3. **Logo and wordmark:** a mark that says both "care, natural" and "thoughtful, premium"
4. **Color system:** natural, but with enough contrast to pop on a shelf
5. **Packaging:** applied to boxes, labels, and bottles
6. **Extended system:** website, social, business cards, hang tags

</Section>

<Section eyebrow="03 · Key decisions" title="Three decisions that built distinctiveness">

<Decision n="01" title="Let the material do the talking">

**What.** The hero of the design was not a photo or an illustration, it was paper and texture. The packaging used stocks you could feel.

**Why.** Everyone else leans on ingredient photos and hand-drawn leaves, which reads earnest but generic. Making the material itself the star sets the brand apart and turns the package into part of the product.

**Tradeoff.** Premium stock costs more to make, but it earns back the premium price and reinforces the "keep it, reuse it" sustainability story.

**Result.** On the shelf, people pick it up just to touch it. The tactile bit becomes part of the brand.

</Decision>

<Decision n="02" title="One color per product, one accent for all">

**What.** A single primary color per product line, plus one shared accent color across everything.

**Why.** The line needed to look like a family and let each product stand on its own. One color each plus a shared accent gives you both unity and variety.

**Tradeoff.** Fewer colors forces bolder choices, but bold and consistent reads far better on a shelf than busy and colorful.

**Result.** The line reads as one brand while each product stays easy to spot.

</Decision>

<Decision n="03" title="Typography as the brand's voice">

**What.** A geometric sans-serif, modern but warm, used everywhere, with custom letterforms for the wordmark.

**Why.** Type carries personality. A geometric sans feels intentional and designed, which matches a brand that is intentional about what it stands for.

**Tradeoff.** Custom type takes care to apply well, but it creates something the brand actually owns.

**Result.** The typography alone signals the values and separates the brand from competitors using off-the-shelf fonts.

</Decision>

</Section>

<Section eyebrow="04 · Solution & artifacts" title="The identity system">

- **Logo:** primary wordmark, monogram, full-color / monochrome / reversed, with spacing and size rules
- **Color:** one primary per line (sage, clay, deep forest) plus a brass-gold accent for unity, all contrast-tested
- **Typography:** a custom geometric sans plus a serif for body copy, with a full scale and weights
- **Primary packaging:** die-cut boxes on textured stock, labels, hang tags, and custom tissue wrap for the unboxing
- **Secondary packaging:** a branded shipping box with an interior note, business cards, stickers
- **Extended system:** website, social, email, and advertising

<Section eyebrow="06 · Reflection" title="Packaging is part of the product">

The experience includes the unboxing: the paper, the design, the little note inside. Constraints made the work better (limiting colors and fonts forced clarity), and the right material choices communicated sustainability without a single "eco" sticker. Once the system existed, rolling it out to new products and channels was the easy part. A good system is reusable and forgiving.
