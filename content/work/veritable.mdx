<Section eyebrow="01 · Context & problem" title="A credible home for a not-very-glamorous job">

Veritable needed a website that felt trustworthy and actually moved people to buy. Later it needed a full move to a new platform. That meant juggling pages for two very different audiences, a real pricing-and-checkout flow, and a big pile of content, all without the whole thing drifting into looking like five different sites.

<InPlainTerms>
Veritable checks whether a patient's insurance will cover a visit before the appointment, so nobody gets a nasty surprise bill weeks later. It is sold both to clinics and to the billing companies behind them (the industry calls those "RCM" companies, short for revenue cycle management, which is a fancy way of saying "the people who get healthcare providers paid").
</InPlainTerms>

</Section>

<Section eyebrow="02 · Role & process" title="Designed it, then built a lot of it">

I built the marketing site and the Code Explorer landing page (layout, theme, content, and a custom icon set called Veritable Legend). I designed the pricing and checkout flow in React, with proper Login and Subscription-Status states. Then I led the 2025 to 2026 migration: re-platformed 12+ page types, reworked the header and menus, moved pricing and social images over, produced the product video, and coordinated CMS updates with engineering.

</Section>

<Section eyebrow="03 · Key decisions" title="Two decisions that defined the build">

<Decision n="01" title="Treated pricing as product UX, not a poster">

**What.** Designed a real plan-to-checkout-to-subscription flow in React, not a static price table with a hopeful "contact us" button.

**Why.** If someone is ready to buy, they should be able to see plans, pick one, pay, and see their status, without going on a scavenger hunt for the buy button.

**Tradeoff.** A live flow with login and subscription states is far more work than a table.

**Result.** Checkout is now a designed experience instead of an afterthought.

</Decision>

<Decision n="02" title="One visual system across a sprawling site">

**What.** Made one set of icons (Veritable Legend), one theme, and one component set, then applied them across 13+ page types.

**Why.** Multi-audience sites tend to look like multiple products bolted together. Consistency quietly signals "one company, one promise."

**Tradeoff.** The system takes time to build up front before it pays off.

**Result.** Every page reads as one coherent product, even though they speak to different people.

</Decision>

</Section>

<Section eyebrow="04 · Solution & artifacts" title="What shipped">

- The marketing site (layout, theme, content)
- The Code Explorer landing page with a custom icon set
- Audience-specific pages (For Practices, For RCM Companies, Eligibility Verification, Code Explorer, Managed Service, a long-form software page, an anchored FAQ)
- A pricing and checkout flow (plans, checkout, login, subscription status)
- A full re-platforming of 12+ page types
- A header and menu redesign
- The product video and thumbnails
- An ad system (GIF and AI-generated video ads in 16:9, 9:16, and 4:5, with sound design)

<Section eyebrow="06 · Reflection" title="Owning a web product end to end">

Veritable is my clearest example of owning a web product from blank page to go-live: the visual system, the information architecture, a working checkout, and the frontend build. Keeping a big, multi-audience site coherent through a full migration taught me how to scale design thinking without letting it fray at the edges.
