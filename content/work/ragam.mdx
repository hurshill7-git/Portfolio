<Section eyebrow="01 · Context & problem" title="Great shows nobody could find">

Independent theatre (small plays, improv, experimental stuff) was basically invisible. It happened in tiny venues, promoted by word of mouth and the occasional buried social post. Anyone who wanted to discover new theatre had nowhere to look, and producers spent hours wrangling tickets and emails by hand.

<InPlainTerms>
This was a two-sided problem. Producers needed a dead-simple way to list a show and sell tickets. Audiences needed a way to find shows near them. Eventbrite was too generic and Broadway sites only cared about the big productions, so the scrappy indie middle had nothing.
</InPlainTerms>

I built Ragam for exactly that middle: effortless for a busy producer to set up, genuinely enjoyable for an audience to browse.

</Section>

<Section eyebrow="02 · Role & process" title="Solo designer, two core flows">

I owned research, flows, interaction, visual design, and dev handoff.

1. **Research:** interviewed real producers and theatre-goers about what they actually needed
2. **User flows:** mapped "Create a Show" and "Find and Book a Show"
3. **Interaction design:** made it feel immediate, not like filing paperwork
4. **Visual design:** theatrical and sophisticated, but still approachable
5. **Handoff:** detailed specs, and iteration through the builds

</Section>

<Section eyebrow="03 · Key decisions" title="Three decisions rooted in real life">

<Decision n="01" title="Make listing a show almost effortless">

**What.** The "Create a Show" flow fits in five screens, with nothing required beyond the essentials.

**Why.** Producers are also doing the lighting, the acting, and the panicking. The faster they can list a show, the more shows exist on the platform.

**Tradeoff.** Fewer required fields means less data, but a listed show beats a perfect-but-never-finished one.

**Result.** Producers list a show in three to five minutes, which keeps supply flowing.

</Decision>

<Decision n="02" title="Search and filters, not a mystery feed">

**What.** A straightforward search-and-filter setup (title, date, genre, distance) instead of an algorithmic recommendation feed.

**Why.** People hunting for theatre roughly know what they want. "Comedy within five miles this weekend" beats an infinite scroll of the app's best guesses.

**Tradeoff.** Feeds feel more addictive, but for this job, useful beats addictive.

**Result.** Audiences find what they want fast, without wading through irrelevant shows.

</Decision>

<Decision n="03" title="Reviews, because theatre is a gamble">

**What.** Every show carries star ratings and written reviews, front and center on its page.

**Why.** A stranger's show could be transcendent or deeply awkward. Reviews take the edge off that bet.

**Tradeoff.** Early shows have no reviews yet, so trust has to build over time.

**Result.** As the platform grows, reviews become the signal that nudges people to buy.

</Decision>

</Section>

<Section eyebrow="04 · Solution & artifacts" title="The core product">

- **Discovery:** search, filters (date, location, genre), a grid of show cards, and a map view
- **Show detail:** hero, description, performer bios, venue, ticket types, reviews, and Book Now
- **Booking:** pick tickets, enter details, pay via Stripe, get a QR code at the door
- **Producer dashboard:** manage shows, see sales and attendance, read reviews, export the guest list

The look leaned into the theatre of it all: deep purple and spotlight gold, a serif for the stage-y feeling, and a clean sans for the actual buttons.

<Section eyebrow="06 · Reflection" title="Remove the obstacles to the core action">

The best features are sometimes the ones you leave out. Every required field in the producer flow was a small wall, and taking the walls down got more shows listed. The hard part was never the producer side, it was building enough shows to make the audience side worth opening. And reviews quietly turned "is this any good?" into "here is what 47 people thought."
