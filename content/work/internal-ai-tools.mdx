<Section eyebrow="01 · Context & problem" title="The same chores, over and over">

Two jobs on the sales side were quietly eating people alive. Every time a new prospect showed up, someone built them a sales deck by hand. No two decks looked alike, and a single one could swallow an afternoon. And every time a client's team structure changed, someone rebuilt the org chart and reporting lines from scratch in a design tool, like repainting a fence that keeps growing new posts.

<InPlainTerms>
"Go-to-market" is just the sales-and-marketing side of the company. The pain was simple: smart people spending hours doing repetitive busywork that a tool could do in minutes. I decided to be that tool.
</InPlainTerms>

I treated these as product problems, not favors. If two people felt this pain, more did. The bottleneck was the process, not anyone's preference.

</Section>

<Section eyebrow="02 · Role & process" title="Find the friction, build the fix, get out of the way">

I spotted the recurring pain, designed the fix, built it, shipped it with a short walkthrough video, and then watched people adopt it on their own.

1. Found the friction (rebuilding decks and charts by hand)
2. Designed the interaction (transcript in, finished deck out; drag-and-drop chart builder)
3. Built the tools (Claude skills and a small web app)
4. Recorded walkthrough videos so anyone could start solo
5. Handed it over with no training session required

</Section>

<Section eyebrow="03 · Key decisions" title="Three calls that drove adoption">

<Decision n="01" title="Designed for self-service, not just function">

**What.** Every tool shipped with a walkthrough video and a copy-paste guide, so a non-designer could use it without me hovering nearby.

**Why.** If a tool needs a training session, only the people forced to use it ever will. If it just works by following a two-minute video, using it becomes a choice, and a choice is the only honest proof that the thing is actually useful.

**Tradeoff.** Making the video and the guide takes real time. Shipping the bare tool is faster.

**Result.** Casey followed the video on May 13, 2026 and it just worked, no meeting needed. Alok got the Chart Builder video on May 27. Mimi asked for access unprompted on May 28 and started requesting features, which is the clearest sign yet that the pain was real.

</Decision>

<Decision n="02" title="Met people where they already were">

**What.** The Chart Builder exports as PNG or JSON and can rebuild a chart from a screenshot, so nobody has to bounce between five apps to get one diagram.

**Why.** The old routine was design tool to spreadsheet to design tool to slides. Every hop was an excuse to just do it manually instead. Remove the hops, remove the excuse.

**Tradeoff.** Screenshot-to-chart conversion, export formats, and autosave are more work to build.

**Result.** Mimi immediately asked whether the output text could be more readable for RFP responses, which means she is using it for real work, not kicking the tires.

</Decision>

<Decision n="03" title="Designed around a trust objection">

**What.** For the call summarizer, I built the "can I really trust AI with a client call?" worry straight into the design: it labels who is from 314e and who is the client, tracks who asked what, and attributes every finding.

**Why.** One stakeholder doubted AI could read a client's tone from a transcript. Rather than argue, I made the tool honest about exactly what it knows and what it does not.

**Tradeoff.** More logic, and more explaining inside the output.

**Result.** The concern got addressed in the design instead of dismissed, and adoption followed because the tool never oversold itself.

</Decision>

</Section>

<Section eyebrow="04 · Solution & artifacts" title="Four tools, shipped">

1. **Deck-automation skill.** Paste a sales-call transcript (Loom, Zoom, Teams) and it fills the Current State, Requirements, and Solution slides automatically. Shipped March 31, 2026.
2. **Call summarizer.** Reads a transcript, tells the 314e voices apart from the client's, pulls out the client's real questions, and writes a tidy executive briefing. Shipped April 9, 2026.
3. **Chart Builder web app.** Drag people into a hierarchy, watch reporting lines route themselves, or paste a screenshot and let it rebuild the chart. Exports cleanly, saves as you go, and never opens a design tool. Shipped May 26, 2026.
4. **Smart Slides app.** A companion for generating and polishing sales decks. Built March 20, 2026.

<Section eyebrow="06 · Reflection" title="What I'd carry forward">

This is the work I am proudest of. I noticed a recurring chore, designed something to kill it, built it, and earned genuine adoption. That is product thinking (problem, audience, distribution, iteration) wearing the clothes of scrappy building.

Next time I would test with two or three people before shipping rather than polishing in private. The big lesson: people adopt a tool when it is easier than the old way, not when they are ordered to.
