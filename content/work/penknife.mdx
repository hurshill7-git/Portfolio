<Section eyebrow="01 · Context & problem" title="Three tools, one product, one identity">

Penknife is really three products in a trench coat, and the job was to explain it clearly on the web without it falling apart into three separate-looking things.

<InPlainTerms>
Penknife is recruiting software with three parts. ATS (an applicant tracking system) manages candidates as they move through hiring. CRM keeps track of relationships and outreach. Sourcing is about finding new candidates in the first place. Three jobs, one platform.
</InPlainTerms>

</Section>

<Section eyebrow="02 · Role & process" title="Page design and a visual language">

I designed the ATS, CRM, and Sourcing pages (layout, formatting, the visual touches that set each one apart), and built the site's illustration and visual language, then kept it consistent across pages, blog thumbnails, and social images.

</Section>

<Section eyebrow="03 · Key decision" title="Distinct per tool, coherent across the platform">

<Decision n="01" title="Each tool its own accent, all one family">

**What.** Each product area got its own visual treatment inside one shared language, so the three read as one platform.

**Why.** People need to feel that each tool does a different job, while trusting it is all the same product they are paying for.

**Tradeoff.** Some up-front system work to keep everything consistent.

**Result.** Three tools that feel distinct but clearly related.

</Decision>

</Section>

<Section eyebrow="05 · Reflection" title="Make the complex legible">

Penknife was an early project, more visual and web design than deep product UX, and there are no launch metrics to point to. But it taught me to make something complicated feel obvious to an outsider, and to build a reusable visual language instead of decorating each page one at a time.
