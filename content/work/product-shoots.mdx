<Section eyebrow="01 · Context" title="Images that sell">

Commercial product photography for e-commerce. The goal is images that actually sell, by pairing technical lighting with art direction that tells a little story about the product, rather than just a clean cutout floating on white.

</Section>

<Section eyebrow="02 · Approach" title="Light, styling, consistency">

**Lighting.** A key, a fill, and a back light, tuned to each product. Soft, diffused light for shiny things like metal and glass; harder light for matte things, to bring out texture. Electronics want clean clarity, fabrics want raking side light, food wants warmth.

<InPlainTerms>
The three-light setup is the quiet backbone of product photography. The "key" is your main light, the "fill" softens the shadows it casts, and the "back light" separates the product from the background so it does not melt into it. Get those three right and the product looks expensive.
</InPlainTerms>

**Composition and styling.** Shoot the product in context. A coffee maker is not just a coffee maker on white, it is a morning kitchen with warm light and a cup nearby. Context makes it aspirational. A few minutes spent on the angle or a prop can be the difference between "neat" and "I want that."

**Post.** Consistent color across the whole set (e-commerce demands it), texture brought forward, distractions removed, but never to the point where the product looks fake. Honesty still matters.

</Section>

<Section eyebrow="03 · Process" title="From brief to delivery">

1. **Pre-shoot:** client brief (what it does, who buys it, what mood), a mood board, a shot list
2. **Setup:** lighting and composition tests
3. **Shoot:** lots of angles and styling variations, 20 to 30 setups per product
4. **Review:** pick the keepers in-camera, move to post
5. **Post:** color, enhancement, retouching
6. **Final:** deliver in the formats the client needs (web, print, packaging)

<Section eyebrow="04 · Why it works" title="Craft meets conversion">

Product photos tie straight to sales: a good one lifts clicks and purchases, a bad one quietly kills them. The work needs technical skill (light, camera, post) and creative skill (art direction, styling), at the speed real budgets demand, often 15 to 20 products in a single day. Clients have reported a clear bump moving from phone photos to professional shoots, though I would treat any exact percentage as their number, not an independently measured one.

</Section>
